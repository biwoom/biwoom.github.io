## 1.1 The Story about the Elder Cakkhupāla Cakkhupālattheravatthu

> **요약**
>
> Dhp 1
> Burlingame: “If Thine Eye Offend Thee, Pluck It Out”
> Compare: Thag-a 95
> The bhikkhu Cakkhupāla determined to fulfil his practice even at the cost of his eyes, and eventually he attained Awakening; the Buddha explained the deed he did in the past which caused his loss of sight in the present, and he summarised the teaching with a verse.
> Cast: Mahā Suvaṇṇa, Cakkhupāla (Mahā Pāla, aka Pālita), Culla Pāla, Anāthapiṇḍika, Visākhā
> Keywords: Rich Men, Devatās, Determination, Sakka, Amanussa, Doctors, Past Lives
> ****

> Mind precedes thoughts, mind is their chief,
> their quality is made by mind,
> if with base mind one speaks or acts
> through that suffering follows one
> like a wheel follows ox’s foot.  *[注: AJ: normally the commentary only includes the first line of the verse, but here, at the beginning of the first story, it quotes the whole verse.]*

Where was this Dhamma teaching given? At Sāvatthī. With reference to whom? The Elder Cakkhupāla.

At Sāvatthī, we are told, lived a householder named Mahā Suvaṇṇa (Great Gold). He was rich, possessed of great wealth, possessed of ample means of enjoyment, but at the same time he was childless. One day, as he was on his way home from bathing at a river, he saw by the roadside a large forest tree with spreading branches. He thought: “This tree must be tenanted by a powerful Devatā.” So he caused the ground under the tree to be cleared, the tree itself to be enclosed with a wall, and sand to be spread within the enclosure. And having decked the tree with flags and banners, he made the following vow: “Should I obtain a son or a daughter, I will pay you great honor.” Having so done, he went on his way.

Now, in no long time his wife conceived a child in her womb.  So soon as his wife knew that she was with child, she informed her husband, and he performed the protection of the womb for her. On the expiration of ten lunar months she gave birth to a son. Since the merchant obtained a son by protecting the tree, he named his son Pāla (Protector).

After a time he obtained a second son. The younger son he named Culla Pāla (Protector Junior), calling the older son Mahā Pāla (Protector Senior). When they reached manhood, their parents obtained wives for them. After a time the mother and father died, leaving the entire estate to be administered by the two sons.

At this time the Teacher, having set in motion the glorious Wheel of the Dhamma,  *[注: AJ: it means after beginning his teaching career with the first discourse. Note the most common apellation for the Buddha used in this text isSattha, Teacher.]* after journeying from place to place, took up his residence at Jetavana, a monastery erected by the wealthy merchant  Anāthapiṇḍika at a cost of 540 millions coins. While in residence at Jetavana, he established the multitude in the way to heaven and the way to deliverance.

For the Realised One kept residence during but a single rainy season at the monastery erected by twice 80,000 families of kinsmen, eighty on his mother’s side and eighty on his father’s. At Jetavana monastery, erected by Anāthapiṇḍika, he kept residence during nineteen rainy seasons; at Pubbārāma, erected by Visākhā at a cost of 270 milllion, he kept residence during six rainy seasons. Thus, by reason of the great merit of these two families, he kept residence near Sāvatthī during twenty-five rainy seasons.

Anāthapiṇḍika and Visākhā, the eminent female lay disciple, regularly went twice every day to wait upon the Realised One. Knowing that the young novices would expect alms from them, they never went empty-handed. Before breakfast  they took food, both hard and soft; after breakfast they took the five medicaments and the eight beverages. Moreover, in their residences seats were always prepared for two thousand bhikkhus. Whoever wished food or drink or medicine was immediately provided with just what he wished.

However, not a single day had Anāthapiṇḍika asked the Teacher a question. Anāthapiṇḍika, we are told, refrained from asking questions by reason of his excessive love for the Teacher. He thought to himself: “The Realised One is a delicate Buddha, a delicate prince. Were the Teacher, because of the thought: ‘This householder is my supporter,’ to teach the Dhamma to me, he would grow weary.” Therefore he asked the Teacher no questions. But so soon as Anāthapiṇḍika took his seat, the Teacher thought to himself: “This merchant protects me where I have no need to be protected. For I spent four immeasurables and 100,000 aeons of time in addition fulfilling the perfections. My own gloriously adorned head have I cut off; my eyes have I torn out; my heart’s flesh have I uprooted; both son and wife, dear to me as life, have I renounced, solely that I might teach the Dhamma to others. This man protects me where I have no need to be protected.” And straightaway he taught a sermon on the Dhamma.

At this time seventy million people dwelt in Sāvatthī. Of these, fifty million became noble disciples after hearing the discourse of the Teacher, but twenty million remained unattained. The noble disciples had two duties: before breakfast they gave alms; after breakfast, bearing perfumes and garlands in their hands, with  servants bearing garments, medicaments, and beverages, they went to hear the Dhamma.

Now one day Mahā Pāla saw the noble disciples going to the monastery with perfumes and garlands in their hands.  When he saw them, he asked: “Where is this great throng going?” – “To hear the Dhamma.” – “I will go too,” he said. So he went, worshipped the Teacher, and sat down in the outer circle of the congregation.

Now when the Buddhas teach the Dhamma, they have regard to the predispositions of their hearers for the refuges, the moral precepts, and going forth. Thus they always teach the Dhamma with reference to the disposition of mind of each individual. When, therefore, the Teacher taught the Dhamma on that day, he had regard to Mahā Pāla’s predispositions. And he taught in orderly sequence, expounding one subject after another; to wit, almsgiving, the moral precepts, heaven, the dangers of sensual pleasures, the baseness of the defilments, and the advantages of going forth.

Mahā Pāla the householder listened. He thought: “When a man goes to the next world, neither sons nor daughters nor riches follow him; nay, even his own body does not go with him. Of what profit is it for me to live the household life? I will go forth.” So at the end of the discourse he approached the Teacher and asked to be given the going forth.  *[注: AJ: Burlingame, here and many other places, has, “…asked to be received into the Order,” or something along the same lines. However, the going forth is the preliminary step, and does not amount to entering the Saṅgha. That only occurs when receiving higher ordination. Therefore throughout Burlingame’s translation there is a confusion in this regard.]* The Teacher asked him: “Have you no kinsman of whom it is proper that you should ask leave?” – “Why yes, venerable Sir, I have a younger brother.” – “Well then, ask him.” To this Mahā Pāla agreed, and said: “Very well.” So he worshipped the Teacher and went home.

Summoning his younger brother, he said to him: “Dear brother, whatever wealth is in this house, whether animate or inanimate, all this I give into your hands; take possession thereof.” – “But you, master?” – “I shall go forth under the Teacher.” – “What say you, dear brother? When my mother died, I gained in you as it were a mother; when my father died, as it were a father. Your house contains great wealth. Surely you can do works of merit even though you live the household life.  Do not so.” – “Dear brother, after hearing the Teacher teach the Dhamma, I can no longer live the life of a householder. For the Teacher taught a Dhamma lovely in its beginning, its middle, and its end, and established precisely and exactly the three characteristics of existing things: impermanence, suffering, and non-self. I cannot fulfill the Dhamma amid the  cares of the household life; I must go forth, dear brother.” – “Dear brother, now you are young. Wait until you are old, and then go forth.” – “Dear brother, in the case of an old man, even hands and feet are disobedient and do not answer to his will; how much more so his kinsmen? No, I will not do as you say; I will fulfill the duties of a bhikkhu.

> Weakened by old age are
> hands and feet, disobedient.
> For the one lacking strength
> how will he live by the Dhamma?

Dear brother, I shall go forth despite all considerations to the contrary.”

In spite of his brother’s lamentations Mahā Pāla went to the Teacher and asked to go forth.  *[注: AJ: this is an anomaly, as he should not have been allowed to go forth without permission.]* He went forth and took higher ordination and spent five rainy seasons in residence with his teachers and preceptor. When he had completed his fifth residence and celebrated the Invitation,  *[注: AJ: i.e. theInvitation(Pavāraṇa)is to other monastics to point out faults seen, heard or suspected.]* he approached the Teacher, worshipped him, and asked: “Venerable Sir, how many duties are there in this religion?” – “Two duties only, bhikkhu: the duty of study and the duty of meditation.” – “Venerable Sir, what is meant by the duty of study, and what is meant by the duty of meditation?” – “The duty of study necessitates gaining a knowledge of the word of the Buddha in a manner conformable to one’s understanding, the mastery of one or two Collections (Nikāya), or indeed of the whole Three Baskets (Tipiṭaka), bearing it in mind, reciting it, teaching it.  On the other hand the duty of meditation, which leads to Arahatship, involves frugal living, satisfaction with a remote lodging, fixing firmly in one’s mind the idea of decay and death, and the development of insight by persistent effort.” – “Venerable Sir, since I went forth in old age, I shall not be able to fulfill the duty of study. But I can fulfill the duty of meditation; teach me a meditation subject.”  *[注: AJ: above his brother had pointed out he was still young, so there seems to be an inconsistency in the story here.]*

So the Teacher taught him a meditation subject leading to Arahatship. Then he worshipped the Teacher, sought bhikkhus to accompany him, and having obtained sixty, departed with them. When he had proceeded a distance of twenty leagues, he arrived at a large border-village, and accompanied by his retinue, entered the village for alms. The inhabitants, observing that the bhikkhus were faithful in the performance of their duties, were favorably disposed to them, provided them with seats, and served them with savoury food. Then they enquired: “Venerable Sirs, where do the noble bhikkhus go?” – “Lay friends, to a suitable retreat.” Then the wise villagers knew  within themselves: “The venerable bhikkhus seek lodgings wherein to spend the rainy season.”

They said: “If the noble bhikkhus would reside here during these three months, we would abide steadfast in the refuges and receive the moral precepts.” The bhikkhus, thinking to themselves: “Through these families we shall effect escape from the round of existences,” gave their consent. The villagers, having obtained the consent of the bhikkhus, proceeded to erect a monastery, building night-quarters and day-quarters, and when it was finished, presented it to the bhikkhus. The bhikkhus resorted regularly to that village only for alms. And a certain physician came to them and offered his services, saying: “Venerable Sirs, where many reside, disease is inevitable. Should sickness arise, pray send me word, and I will prescribe remedies for you.”

When the bhikkhus entered upon residence on the first day of the rainy season, the elder, addressing them, asked this question:  “Friends, in how many postures will you spend these three months?” – “In all four postures, venerable Sir.” – “But, friends, is this proper? Assuredly we must be heedful, for it was from the living Buddha that we received our meditation subject on coming here; and the favor of the Buddhas may not be won by double-dealing, but only by the manifestation of upright intent. Four states of suffering await he who is heedless, that he may enter therein as into his own habitation. Therefore, friends, be heedful.” – “But you, venerable Sir?” – “I shall spend the time in the three postures; I shall not stretch out my back, friends.” – “Very well, venerable Sir. Be heedful.”

*[이미지: A Vow of Heedfulness]*

At the end of the first month the elder, who allowed himself no sleep, began to suffer from an affliction of the eyes. Streams of tears trickled from his eyes, as streams of water from a broken jar. All night long he devoted himself to meditation, and with the coming of dawn he entered his cell and sat down. When it was time for the bhikkhus to go the rounds for alms, they came to the elder and said to him: “Venerable Sir, it is time for us to go the rounds for alms.” – “Very well, friends; take bowl and robe.” Having thus directed them to take their own bowls and robes, he himself set out. The bhikkhus observed that his eyes were running and asked him: “What is the matter, venerable Sir?” – “The wind cuts my eyes, friends.” – “Were we not offered the services of a physician, venerable Sir? We will inform him.” – “Very well,  friends.”

They informed the physician, who prepared an ointment and sent it to the elder. The elder applied the ointment, remaining seated as he did so, and then entered the village. The physician, seeing him, said to him: “Venerable Sir, I am informed that the wind hurts your reverence’s eyes.” – “That is true, lay disciple.” – “Venerable Sir, did you apply an ointment which I prepared and sent you?” – “Yes, lay disciple.” – “How do you feel now?” – “The pain continues just the same, lay disciple.” The physician thought to himself: “The ointment which I sent him should have cured him with only one application. How is it that he is not cured?” So he asked the elder: “Were you seated when you applied the ointment, or were you lying down?” The elder remained silent. Though the physician repeated the question several times, he answered not a word. The physician thought to himself: “I will go to the monastery and have a look at his cell.” So he dismissed the elder, saying to him: “That will do, venerable Sir.”

Going to the monastery, he inspected the elder’s cell. Seeing only a place to walk and a place to sit down, but no place to lie down, he asked the elder: “Venerable Sir, were you seated when you applied the ointment, or were you lying down?” The elder remained silent. “Venerable Sir, do not act in this way; the duties of a religious can be performed only so long as the body is properly cared for. Were you lying down when you applied the ointment?” After the physician had repeated the question several times, the elder replied: “Go your way, friend; I will take counsel and decide the matter for myself.”

Now the elder had no kinsmen or blood-relatives there. With whom, therefore, was he to take counsel? Therefore he took counsel with his own person, saying:  “Come now, brother Pālita, tell me this. Will you give regard to your eyes or to the dispensation of the Buddha? For in the round of existences without conceivable beginning, there is no counting the number of times you have been without eyes. But while unnumbered hundreds of Buddhas and thousands of Buddhas have passed, your experience does not cover the period of even a single Buddha. Now in this rainy season you resolved not to lie down for three months. Therefore let your eyes perish or decay. Keep only the dispensation of the Buddha, not your eyes.” And admonishing his own physical body, he uttered the following verses:

> My eyes decline, my ears decline,
> and likewise also my body;
> all that depends on the body declines
> why, Pālita, are you heedless?

> My eyes decay, my ears decay,
> and likewise also my body;
> all that depends on the body decays
> why, Pālita, are you heedless?

> My eyes will fail, my ears will fail,
> and likewise also my body;
> all that depends on the body will fail
> why, Pālita, are you heedless?

Having thus admonished himself in three verses, he applied the ointment to his nose, remaining seated as before, and then entered the village for alms. The physician, seeing him, asked him: “Venerable Sir, have you applied the ointment?” – “Yes, lay disciple.” – “How do you feel?” – “The pain continues just the same, lay disciple.” – “Venerable Sir, were you seated when you applied the ointment, or were you lying down?” The elder remained silent. The physician repeated the question several times, but the elder answered never a word. Then the physician said to him: “You are not doing as you ought for your own good. Henceforth do not say: ‘So and So prepared ointment for me’ and I will not say: ‘I prepared ointment for you.’”

Given up on by the physician, the elder went to the monastery. He said: “Bhikkhu, though you have been given up by the physician, do not give up your posture.

> Being refused medicine,
> rejected by the doctor,
> destined for the King of Death,
> why, Pālita, be heedless?”

Having admonished himself in this verse, he resumed his meditations. At the end of the middle watch his eyes and his pollutants were blotted out simultaneously, and he became an Arahat through pure insight.  *[注: AJ:Sukkavipassaka.]* He entered his cell and sat down.

*[이미지: Blindness and Insight]*

When the time came for the bhikkhus to go the rounds for alms, they came to the elder and said to him: “Venerable Sir, it is time for us to go the rounds for alms.” – “Is it time, friends?” – “Yes, venerable Sir.” – “Well then, go your way.” – “But you, venerable Sir?” – “The sight of my eyes is gone, friends.” They looked at his eyes, and their own eyes filled with tears. “Do not worry, venerable Sir;  we will look after you,” they said to the elder, comforting him. And having performed the various duties required of them, they entered the village for alms.

Not seeing the elder, people asked the bhikkhus: “Friends, where is our noble elder?” When they learned what had happened, they sent rice-porridge to him. Afterwards, taking food, they went in person,  worshipped the elder, and rolling on the ground before his feet, poured out their lamentations. Then they comforted him, saying: “We will care for you, venerable Sir; do not worry,” and went their way. From that time on they sent rice-porridge regularly to the monastery.

The elder constantly admonished the other sixty bhikkhus, and they carried out his admonitions so faithfully that at the approaching Invitation (*Pavāraṇā*) all of them became Arahats possessed of the analytic knowledges (*paṭisambhidā*). At the end of the rainy season, desiring to see the Teacher, they said to the elder: “Venerable Sir, we desire to see the Teacher.” When the elder heard their request, he thought to himself: “I am weak, and on the way is a forest haunted by Amanussa (Non-Human Beings). If I go with them, all will become weary and will be unable to obtain alms. I will send them on ahead.”

So he said to them: “Friends, you go on ahead.” – “But you, venerable Sir?” – “I am weak, and on the way is a forest haunted by Amanussa. If I go with you, you will all become weary; therefore you go on ahead.” – “Do not so, venerable Sir; we will go only with you.” – “Friends, please do not do so; if you do so, it will displease me. When my younger brother sees you and asks after me, tell him that  I have lost the sight of my eyes, and he will send someone to guide my steps. Greet in my name the One of Ten Strengths  *[注: AJ:Dasabala, i.e. the Buddha.]* and the eighty great elders.” So saying, he dismissed them.

They begged the elder to pardon them for their insistence, and entered the village for alms. The villagers provided them with seats, presented them with alms, and asked them: “Venerable Sirs, may we know why the noble bhikkhus are leaving?” – “Yes, lay disciples, we desire to see the Teacher.” The villagers repeatedly begged the bhikkhus to remain, but finding that they were firm in their determination to go, accompanied them on their way weeping, and then turned back.

After journeying from place to place, the bhikkhus arrived at Jetavana and greeted the Teacher and the eighty great elders in the name of the elder. Having done so, they entered the street where the elder’s younger brother lived for alms. The householder recognized them, received them cordially, provided them with seats, and asked them: “Where is my dear brother the elder?” They told him what had happened. Flinging himself at their feet, he rolled on the ground and wept.

Then he asked them: “Now, friends, what is to be done?” – “The elder wishes to have someone come from here, that he may return  with him.” – “Friends, here is my sister’s son Pālita. Send him.” – “It will never do to send him, for there is danger by the way. We might, however, send him, after first letting him go forth.” – “Do so, and then send him, friends.” So they let him go forth and for a fortnight instructed him in such matters as the proper manner of putting on the robe. Then, showing him the way, they sent him forth.

After journeying from place to place, he arrived at the village. Seeing an old man at the village gate, he asked him: “Is there a forest hermitage near this village?” – “There is, venerable Sir.” – “Who lives there?” – “An elder named Pālita, venerable Sir.” – “Show me the way there.” – “Who are you, venerable Sir?” – “I am the son of the elder’s sister.” So the old man took him and  led him to the hermitage. He worshipped the elder and for a fortnight performed the major and minor duties for him, ministering to him faithfully.

Then he said to him: “Venerable Sir, the householder, my mother’s brother, desires to have you come to him. Let us go there.” – “Very well, take hold of my staff.” Taking hold of the staff by the tip, he entered the village with the elder. The villagers provided the elder with a seat and asked him: “Venerable Sir, may we know your purpose in going?” – “Yes, lay disciples, I am going to pay my respects to the Teacher.” The villagers sought by all means in their power to persuade them to remain, but failing in their efforts, escorted them part of the way, and then turned back weeping.

When the novice had gone part of the way with the elder, holding the tip of the elder’s staff, he arrived at a forest village named Kaṭṭhanagara (Wood Town), near which the elder formerly resided. As the novice came out of the village, he heard in the forest the voice of a woman singing away as she gathered firewood. As he listened to her song, he fell in love with her voice.

There is no sound to be compared with a woman’s voice for power to thrill man’s whole frame. Therefore said the Fortunate One: “Bhikkhus, I know of no other single sound which so completely takes possession of the heart of a man as this, bhikkhus; namely, a woman’s voice.”  *[注: BG: AN 1.1.]*

The novice, fascinated by her voice, let go his hold of the elder’s staff. He said: “Wait just a moment, venerable Sir; I have some business.” So saying,  he went in the direction of the woman. When she saw him, she became silent. The novice violated the law of  chastity with her. The elder thought to himself: “Just now I heard the sound of someone singing, and it was none other than a woman’s voice. The novice tarries; he must have violated the law of chastity.”

When the novice had finished his business, he returned to the elder and said: “Come, venerable Sir, let us be off.” But the elder asked him: “Novice, have you done wrong?” The novice remained silent, and though questioned repeatedly, answered never a word. Then said the elder to him: “A wrongdoer like you can never hold the tip of my staff.”

The novice, overwhelmed with remorse, removed his yellow robes, clothed himself in the garb of a householder, and said: “Venerable Sir, before I was a novice; now I have become a lay disciple again. It was not through faith that I went forth, but because I feared the dangers of the journey. Come, let us be off.”

The elder replied: “A wrongdoer is a wrongdoer, be he lay disciple or be he novice. While you were a novice, you were unable to keep the law of chastity. Will you be a better man for having become a lay disciple? A wrongdoer like you can never hold the tip of my staff.” – “Venerable Sir, the road is infested with Amanussa and you are blind. How can you remain here?” The elder answered: “Friend, don’t worry about that. No matter whether I lie down right here and die, or wander here and there, with you I will never go.” So saying, he pronounced the following verses:

> Alas! I have lost the sight of my eyes,
> across a desert I have come,
> lying down and going no farther,
> with fools no fellowship may be.

> Alas! I have lost the sight of my eyes,
> across a desert I have come,
> I will die and go no farther,
> with fools no fellowship may be.

When the novice heard this, he was overwhelmed with remorse. And he cried out: “A grievous sin indeed have I committed, a deed of savagery and impropriety!” And wringing his hands and weeping, he plunged into the forest and made off.

By the power of the elder’s virtue the Yellowstone Throne of Sakka, the Lord of the Devas, sixty leagues long, fifty leagues wide, fifteen leagues thick, of the color of the China Rose flower, which has a way of lowering itself when Sakka sits down and of rising again when he stands up, manifested signs of heat. “Who, pray, can be seeking to thrust me from my seat?” thought Sakka. Surveying the world with his Divine Eye, he saw the elder. Therefore those of old time said:

> The Lord of the Devas, the thousand-eyed,
> purified the Divine Eye
> condemning wickedness, Pāla
> purified his livelihood.

> The Lord of the Devas, the thousand-eyed,
> purified the Divine Eye
> respecting the Dhamma, Pāla
> purified his livelihood.

Then this thought occurred to him: “Should I fail to go to the assistance of such a one, who blamed wickedness, a Dhamma-revering elder, my head is likely to split into seven pieces. I will go to him.” And so

> The Lord of the Devas, the thousand-eyed,
> bearing glory in his sovereignty,
> having approached in an instant,
> came close unto Cakkhupāla.

Accordingly Sakka approached the elder. When he was quite near him, he shuffled his feet. “Who is there?” asked the elder. “It is I, venerable Sir, a traveler.” – “Where are you going, lay disciple?” – “To Sāvatthī, venerable Sir.” – “Continue your journey, friend.” – “But, venerable Sir, where is your reverence going?” – “I am going there too.” – “Well then, let us go together, venerable Sir.” – “I am weak, friend. If you go with me, you will be delayed.” – “I have no urgent business. Besides, if I go with you, I can avail myself of one of the ten ways and means of acquiring merit. Let us go together, venerable Sir.”

The elder thought to himself: “This is without doubt some pious man.” So he said to him: “Very well, take hold of the tip of my staff, lay brother.” Sakka did so. And Sakka shortened the distance so that they arrived at Jetavana at eventide. The elder, hearing the noise of trumpets, drums, and other instruments of music, asked: “Where is that noise?” – “At Sāvatthī, venerable Sir.” – “Lay brother, when I came here before, we were a long time in coming.” – “I know a short cut, venerable Sir.” At that moment the elder perceived within himself: “This is no human being; it must be a Deva.”

> The Lord of the Devas, the thousand-eyed,
> bearing glory in his sovereignty,
> after having shortened the path,
> quickly came unto Sāvatthī.

Sakka conducted the elder to a hut of leaves and grass which his younger brother had made for his express use,  seated him on a couch, and then, disguising himself as a dear friend of the younger brother, went to summon him. “Friend Pāla!” he called out. “What is it, friend?” – “Do you know that the elder has arrived?” – “No. Is it true that the elder has arrived?” – “Yes, friend, I have just returned from the hermitage, and saw the elder seated in the hut of leaves and grass you built for him.” So saying, he departed.

The householder went to the hermitage. When he saw the elder, he flung himself at his feet, rolled on the ground, and wept. Then he said: “I knew this would happen, venerable Sir. It was for this reason that I withheld from you my permission to go forth.” After talking with him for some time, he freed two slave-boys, had the elder let them go forth, and committed him to their care, saying: “Bring rice porridge and other kinds of food from the village and minister to the elder.” The novices ministered to the elder, performing the major and minor duties faithfully.

Now one day a party of bhikkhus residing in foreign parts came to Jetavana to see the Teacher. After paying their respects to the Teacher and seeing the eighty great elders, they made the rounds of the monastery. Coming to Cakkhupāla’s retreat, they said to each other: “Let us see him too.” So when evening came, they set out to visit him. Just at that moment a severe storm arose. So they turned back, saying: “It is now evening, and a storm has arisen. Therefore we will go and see him in the morning.” The rain continued during the first watch, but ceased in the second. The elder, a man of great energy, accustomed to walking, came down into the cloister in the last watch.

Now at that time many insects had come out of the newly wet earth,  and as the elder walked up and down, they perished in great numbers. The resident bhikkhus did not sweep betimes where the elder walked. When the visiting bhikkhus arrived, saying: “We would see the place where the elder resides,” and saw the insects in the cloister, they asked: “Who was it that walked in this cloister?” – “Our master, venerable Sirs.” They were offended and said: “See what the bhikkhu has done. When he had the sight of his eyes, he lay down and slept and did no wrong. But now that he has lost his eyesight, saying to himself: ‘I will take a walk,’ he has destroyed these insects. ‘That which is right I will do,’ said he; but that which was not right he has done.”

So they went and reported the matter to the Realised One, saying: “Venerable Sir, the Elder Cakkhupāla, saying to himself: ‘I will take a walk,’ has destroyed many insects.” – “But did you see him killing them?” – “We did not, venerable Sir.” – “Precisely as you did not see him, so also did he not see these insects. Bhikkhus, they who have destroyed the pollutants have no thought of killing.” – “Venerable Sir, seeing that he was destined to become an Arahat, how was it that he became blind?” – “Bhikkhus, it was by reason of his misdeed in a former existence.” – “Why, venerable Sir, what did he do?” – “Well then, bhikkhus, listen.”

### 1a Story of the Past: The Wicked Physician and the Woman

In times long past, when the king of Kāsi reigned at Benāres, a certain physician went through towns and villages practicing his profession. Seeing a certain woman with weak eyes, he asked her: “What is the matter with you?” – “My eyesight has failed.” – “I will prescribe for you.” – “Do so, master.” – “What will you give me?” – “If you succeed in making my eyes well and strong again, I will become your slave, and my sons and daughters too.” – “Very well,” said he. So he prescribed a remedy for her, and with a single application of the remedy her eyes became well and strong again.

Upon this she thought: “I promised to become his slave, and my sons and daughters too. But he will not treat me kindly. Therefore I will deceive him.” So when the physician came and asked her how she was getting on, she answered: “Before, my eyes pained me a little; but now they hurt me worse than ever.” The physician thought: “This woman is deceiving me because she is unwilling to give me anything. I don’t want her fee; now I will make her blind.” So he went home and told his wife about the matter. His wife said nothing. Then he compounded an ointment, went to the woman’s house, and directed her to rub it into her eyes. She did so, and her eyes went out like the flame of a lamp. That physician was Cakkhupāla.

“Bhikkhus, the wicked deed then committed by my son followed him ever after; for a wicked deed follows the wrongdoer even as a wheel follows the hoof of the ox that bears the yoke.” After relating this story, the Dhamma king joined the connection, even as a king seals an edict with the royal seal after the clay has been affixed, and pronounced the following verse:  *[注: AJ: I include the Pāḷi as well as the translation.]*

> **1. Manopubbaṅgamā dhammā, manoseṭṭhā manomayā,
> manasā ce paduṭṭhena bhāsati vā karoti vā,
> tato naṁ dukkham-anveti cakkaṁ va vahato padaṁ.**

> Mind precedes thoughts, mind is their chief,
> their quality is made by mind,
> if with base mind one speaks or acts
> through that, suffering follows one
> like a wheel follows ox’s foot.

At the end of the verse 30,000 bhikkhus became Arahats with the analytic knowledges. This teaching that instilled fear was of benefit and fruitful to those assembled.  *[注: AJ: Burlingame omitted this result, and most of the other results of the Buddha’s teaching.]*
